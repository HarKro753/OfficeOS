# Message Inevitability

Critique and rewrite startup messaging so the intended conclusion becomes hard to miss. The goal is not prettier copy. The goal is to remove hidden founder assumptions, vague abstractions, and ambiguous terms until a smart reader reaches the same idea the founder intended without needing extra explanation.

This skill is inspired by the principle behind the Andrew Carnegie-style quote the user referenced: do not make the audience think their way through your meaning. Make understanding feel inevitable.

## Core Standard

A message is inevitable when:

- A smart reader can explain the product, customer, problem, value, and strategic bet after one pass
- Key words point to one intended interpretation, not several plausible ones
- Every abstract claim is grounded in a concrete mechanism, proof point, example, or contrast
- The reader does not need private founder context to connect the dots
- The next belief or action follows naturally from what was said

A message is not inevitable when it depends on:

- Buzzwords that could fit many startups
- Category labels without buyer context
- Benefits without a mechanism
- Vision language without a near-term wedge
- Big market claims without a specific adoption path
- Product claims that assume the reader already understands the workflow
- Ambiguous verbs like "empower", "unlock", "transform", "reimagine", "streamline", "connect", "optimize", or "enable" without a concrete object and outcome

## Language

Default output language is **English**. If the user writes in another language or explicitly requests one, use that language for all outputs instead.

Preserve the user's tone when possible, but do not preserve ambiguity. If the tone conflicts with clarity, clarity wins.

## How It Works

```
INTAKE -> AMBIGUITY SCAN -> READER MODEL -> INEVITABILITY CRITIQUE -> REWRITE -> STRESS TEST
```

This skill can work from any amount of input: a single sentence, a pitch script, landing-page copy, a deck outline, a memo, or positioning material. If the user gives enough text, proceed directly. Ask questions only when the missing context would make critique speculative.

## Phase 1: Intake

This skill is self-contained. Do not search the project for prior startup artifacts, session outputs, or related files on your own. Expect the relevant information to be brought to the skill by the user.

### Check for Provided Material

First inspect what the user has already given:

- Existing pitch, one-liner, deck, landing-page copy, or narrative
- Product description
- Target customer
- Competitive alternatives
- Desired audience reaction
- Current positioning or category
- Specific concerns the user wants tested

If the user provides a document path, read it. If the user provides pasted text, work from the pasted text.

### Ask Only If Needed

If the pitch material is missing or too thin to judge, ask at most four questions:

- What is the product in one sentence?
- Who must understand and believe this message?
- What should they believe after reading it?
- What action should they take next?

Do not over-interview. This skill is primarily a critique and sharpening process, not discovery.

## Phase 2: Ambiguity Scan

Read the message once as a cold investor, buyer, or partner. Mark every word, phrase, or claim that creates optional interpretation.

### Ambiguity Categories

Use these labels:

- **Undefined Category** - The reader cannot tell what kind of thing this is
- **Vague Customer** - The buyer or user could mean too many groups
- **Abstract Problem** - The pain is conceptual rather than observable
- **Unproven Superlative** - "Best", "first", "only", "fastest", or similar without support
- **Benefit Without Mechanism** - The outcome is claimed but the product logic is missing
- **Mechanism Without Outcome** - The product action is described but the value is not
- **Vision Without Wedge** - The long-term ambition is clear, but the entry point is not
- **Jargon Compression** - Too many ideas are packed into insider terms
- **Metric Fog** - Numbers appear without baseline, comparison, or implication
- **Actor Confusion** - It is unclear who does what: user, buyer, customer, system, team, market
- **Pronoun Drift** - "We", "they", "it", "this", or "that" refers to changing things
- **False Familiarity** - The message assumes the reader knows the workflow, market, or pain already

### Output Format

For each issue, capture:

- Exact phrase
- Ambiguity category
- Plausible misread
- Why that misread matters
- What must be made explicit

Be strict. The point is to find places where the intended idea can leak.

## Phase 3: Reader Model

Define what the target reader can and cannot be assumed to know.

### Reader Assumption Table

Create a compact table:

| Assumption                           | Safe To Assume? | Reason          |
| ------------------------------------ | --------------- | --------------- |
| They know the category               | Yes/No/Partial  | Explain briefly |
| They feel the problem                | Yes/No/Partial  | Explain briefly |
| They know current alternatives       | Yes/No/Partial  | Explain briefly |
| They understand why now              | Yes/No/Partial  | Explain briefly |
| They believe the solution can work   | Yes/No/Partial  | Explain briefly |
| They know why this team/product wins | Yes/No/Partial  | Explain briefly |

If the user did not specify the audience, infer the most likely audience from the material and label it as an assumption.

## Phase 4: Inevitability Critique

Judge whether the message forces the intended belief chain.

### Belief Chain

Extract or reconstruct the intended chain:

1. The world has changed or a painful status quo exists
2. A specific customer has a specific problem
3. Existing alternatives fail in a specific way
4. This product has a concrete mechanism that solves it
5. The result is valuable enough to switch, buy, invest, or care
6. The company has a credible wedge or unfair reason to win

For each link, rate:

- **Inevitable** - The reader almost certainly gets it
- **Likely** - The reader probably gets it, but one phrase could be clearer
- **Fragile** - The reader may get it only with context
- **Missing** - The message does not establish it

### Severity Levels

- **Red Flag** - Causes a materially wrong or weak interpretation
- **Yellow Flag** - Creates friction, delay, or avoidable questions
- **Green Flag** - Clear, concrete, and hard to misread

Lead with the highest-severity issues. Do not bury major clarity failures under copy suggestions.

## Phase 5: Rewrite

Rewrite only after critique. The rewrite must make each important thought explicit enough that the reader cannot reasonably wander elsewhere.

### Rewrite Rules

- Replace abstractions with concrete nouns, actors, actions, and outcomes
- Tie every major benefit to a mechanism
- Name the customer narrowly enough to picture them
- Name the current alternative or status quo
- Use contrast to make the difference obvious
- Keep the vision, but anchor it in a wedge
- Prefer one clean claim over three adjacent claims
- Avoid inflated language unless proof is present

### Required Rewrite Outputs

Provide the smallest useful set based on the user's input:

- **Clean One-Liner** - One sentence that says product, customer, problem, and value
- **Investor Version** - Clearer narrative for fundraising or strategic readers
- **Buyer Version** - Clearer narrative for customers or users
- **Vision Version** - Ambition stated with a concrete wedge
- **Before/After Table** - Original phrase, problem, replacement, reason

If the user only asks for critique, do not force a full rewrite. Provide concise replacement options for the worst phrases.

## Phase 6: Stress Test

Run the final message through these tests.

### The Smart Stranger Test

Could a smart person outside the founder's head answer these after one pass?

- What is it?
- Who is it for?
- What painful thing changes?
- Why is the current way insufficient?
- How does the product create the outcome?
- Why should this exist now?
- Why might this company win?

### The Misread Test

List the top 3 ways the message could still be misunderstood. For each, either:

- Fix the copy, or
- State that the risk is acceptable and why

### The Inevitability Score

Score the message from 1-10:

- **1-3** - Mostly founder-context-dependent
- **4-6** - Understandable, but several meanings remain optional
- **7-8** - Clear enough for most readers, minor friction remains
- **9-10** - The intended meaning is highly constrained and easy to repeat

Do not give a 9 or 10 unless the message is specific, concrete, and repeatable.

## Output Styles

### Quick Critique

Use for short user messages or fast reviews:

1. One-sentence verdict
2. Top 3 ambiguity issues
3. Best rewrite
4. Inevitability score

### Full Audit

Use for pitch decks, long narratives, landing pages, or investor materials:

1. Executive verdict
2. Reader model
3. Ambiguity table
4. Belief-chain assessment
5. Highest-risk misreads
6. Rewrite recommendations
7. Final tightened version
8. Inevitability score

## Quality Bar

Be objective and unsentimental. The user is asking for critique, not affirmation.

Do not say a phrase is "clear enough" if it only feels clear because the founder explained it elsewhere. Do not accept startup vocabulary as meaning. Force every important claim to answer: "What would the reader concretely picture, believe, or do differently?"

The final message should sound like the company understands its own thought so well that the audience has no work left to do.
